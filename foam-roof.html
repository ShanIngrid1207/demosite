Why Foam

Foam is the right answer for almost every flat roof in the East Valley.

If your East Valley home has a flat or low-slope section — a custom build, a back patio addition, an accessory dwelling, an older ranch — there's a strong chance it's foam. Spray polyurethane foam (SPF) has been the go-to flat-roof system in Arizona for decades because it does three things really well in our climate: it reflects heat, it's seamless (no joints to fail), and it can be recoated indefinitely instead of replaced.

Foam works in two parts. The base layer is closed-cell polyurethane foam — sprayed on as a liquid, it expands and hardens in seconds. Over the foam goes a reflective elastomeric coating, usually white, which protects the foam from UV and bounces heat back away from the home. The coating is the wear layer; when it gets thin (around year 5–10 in Arizona sun), you recoat it. The foam underneath stays good for decades.

We do new foam installs, recoats on existing foam systems, and leak repairs. Most of our recoat work is on systems that were originally installed in the 1990s or 2000s and are still going strong with periodic maintenance.

Why foam works so well in Arizona

Most flat-roof systems were developed in cooler, wetter climates. Single-ply membranes (TPO, EPDM) work fine but have seams every 6–10 feet, and seams are where flat roofs fail. Built-up roofs (BUR) with hot tar and gravel are old-school, heavy, and don't reflect heat. Foam was developed largely for the southwest because it solves both problems at once.

The reflectivity matters more than people realize. A black single-ply roof in Phoenix surface-temperature can hit 180°F. A white-coated foam roof in the same conditions hovers around 100–115°F. That's a meaningful difference in attic load, AC bills, and roof system lifespan.

Insulation is the other big advantage. Foam is itself an insulator — typical SPF roofing systems add R-6 to R-7 per inch of foam thickness. A standard 1.5-inch foam roof adds R-9 to R-10 of insulation right at the roof deck, on top of whatever's in the attic. For older East Valley homes with marginal attic insulation, this can drop summer cooling costs noticeably.

The foam system, layer by layer

  • 01

    Roof deck preparation

    Existing roof condition is assessed. On a recoat, the existing foam is cleaned and prepped. On a new install over an existing system, prep depends on what's there.

  • 02

    Foam application

    Two-part polyurethane is sprayed through specialized equipment. The chemicals mix at the gun, expand 30x as they hit the surface, and harden within seconds. Standard residential thickness is 1.5 inches.

  • 03

    Base coat

    Elastomeric coating sprayed over the foam. Provides UV protection and the first layer of waterproofing.

  • 04

    Granule embed (optional but recommended)

    Roofing granules broadcast into the wet base coat. Adds walkability and additional UV protection.

  • 05

    Top coat

    Final reflective elastomeric layer. Bright white for maximum reflectivity.

The whole process is usually 1–2 days for a typical East Valley foam install or recoat, depending on roof size and weather.

Recoats — what makes foam different

This is the key advantage of foam over other flat roof systems. The foam itself doesn't really wear out — closed-cell SPF maintains structural integrity for decades. What wears out is the protective coating on top. Arizona UV slowly degrades the elastomeric, and when it gets thin (typically year 5–10 depending on the original coating thickness and exposure), it's time to recoat.

A recoat means cleaning the existing surface, addressing any minor damage, and applying a fresh coating system. Cost is a fraction of a new roof. With consistent recoat maintenance every 5–10 years, the same foam roof can last 30–50+ years before any major work is needed.

Recoat timing: Don't wait for leaks. By the time foam starts leaking, the coating has been gone long enough that the foam itself has UV damage. Recoat at year 5–10 (depending on system) and you preserve the substrate. Wait until year 12+ and you're often into partial foam replacement, not just recoating.
