East Valley Tile

The tile is fine. The underlayment is what fails.

Concrete tile is one of the longest-lasting roofing materials made — 50+ years isn't unusual. Clay tile lasts longer than that. So when a tile roof leaks, the question almost always isn't "do I need new tile?" It's "is the underlayment shot?"

The underlayment is the waterproof sheet beneath the tiles — black felt on older roofs, synthetic on newer ones. It's what actually keeps water out. The tiles shed most of the water, but anything that gets past them lands on the underlayment. Arizona heat bakes the underlayment brittle starting around year 15. By year 20, it's typically failing.

What we do on most East Valley tile jobs: tiles come off carefully and stack on pallets, the old underlayment gets ripped off, decking gets inspected and repaired if needed, new high-temp underlayment goes down, and the original tiles go back up. Broken tiles get replaced with color matches. The roof looks the same when it's done — but it's a fresh 20-year clock instead of a leak waiting to happen.

The tile roofing situation in the East Valley

Drive through almost any East Valley subdivision built between 1995 and 2012 and the roofs are concrete tile. Sometimes flat tile, mostly profiled (the curved ones — S-tile or W-tile). The reason is simple: HOA architectural guidelines required it, and tile holds up well to AZ sun.

Newer subdivisions (post-2015) split — some still tile, some moved to architectural shingle for cost. But if your East Valley home was built between 2000 and 2010, there's a 70%+ chance you have concrete tile. And that puts you smack in the underlayment-replacement window right now.

Concrete tile vs clay tile

Concrete tile

What 90%+ of East Valley homes have. Manufactured by companies like Boral, Eagle, Westile, MonierLifetile. Cost-effective, 50+ year service life on the tile itself, comes in dozens of color blends to match HOA palettes. Heavy — needs structural roof support, which is built into all newer Arizona construction.

Clay tile

Less common in production subdivisions, more common in custom homes and Spanish-style architecture. Lifespan 75–100 years. Higher cost. Available in true terracotta as well as glazed colors. Often seen on premium custom homes in north Gilbert, southeast Mesa, and parts of Apache Junction.

Both materials use the same underlayment systems beneath, so the maintenance picture is similar. The tile lasts the life of the home; the underlayment is the wear item.

Underlayment replacement — what it actually involves

  • 01

    Tile removal

    Tiles come off in pieces — ridge tiles first, then field tiles. They're stacked on pallets on the roof or moved to the ground. We label sections so they go back in the right places.

  • 02

    Old underlayment tear-off

    The brittle, often-disintegrating felt or synthetic comes off. The roof deck is exposed.

  • 03

    Decking inspection

    We check every section for soft spots, water damage, or rot. Damaged plywood gets replaced. Itemized separately if found — most East Valley homes need 0 to 4 sheets.

  • 04

    New underlayment install

    High-temp synthetic underlayment rated for Arizona heat. Better than the original 1990s/2000s felt.

  • 05

    New flashing

    All flashing replaced — pipe boots, valley metal, wall flashing, ridge venting. The point of the project is starting fresh, not reusing old failed parts.

  • 06

    Tile re-lay

    Tiles go back in their original positions. Broken tiles get replaced with matched colors. Ridge caps are re-bedded with fresh mortar.

  • 07

    Final cleanup and walkthrough

    Magnet sweep for nails, photo documentation, warranty paperwork.

Total time: typically 3–4 days on a standard 2,000–3,000 sq ft single-story East Valley home. Two-story or cut-up roofs take longer.

Tile underlayment replacement cost in the East Valley

  • Standard tile underlayment replacement: $11,000 – $20,000
  • Premium underlayment systems (longer warranty): $14,000 – $24,000
  • New tile install (when original tiles can't be reused): $18,000 – $32,000
  • Decking replacement (if needed): $80–$140 per sheet, itemized separately
  • Tile-only repair (a few broken tiles): $300–$1,500

Pitch, square footage, number of roof penetrations, two-story access, and HOA color requirements all affect the final number. Every quote is itemized and in writing.

If you have a leak right now: We can do an emergency repair to stop active water intrusion while HOA approval is in progress. The repair gets credited toward the full underlayment job if you proceed.

HOA approval — we handle the paperwork

Almost every East Valley HOA requires architectural approval before re-roofing. The two big requirements:

  • Tile color matching. Most HOAs maintain an approved color palette. We submit a tile sample with your application.
  • Material specifications. Type of underlayment, manufacturer, warranty terms.

HOA approval typically takes 2–4 weeks. We start the paperwork as soon as you sign the proposal so it's not on the critical path.

Brands and materials we use

For underlayment, we install high-temp synthetic systems engineered for tile roofing. These are significantly more durable than the original 1990s/2000s felt. Common systems include Polyglass, GAF Tiger Paw HT, and Owens Corning ProArmor.

For new tile installs, we work with all major Western manufacturers including Eagle Roofing Products, Boral, and others. Color match for HOA submissions is part of the proposal process.
